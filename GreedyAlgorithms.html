Greedy Algorithm

Overview

For the greedy portion of our COMPS, we redesign both in-class activities and a larger programming assignment. By the two projects, we want students to view greedy algorithms not as purely mathematical procedures but as choices shaped by human values.

In many courses, greedy algorithms are presented as clean optimization: identify the “best” local move, prove it’s optimal, and proceed. However, our project want students to learn “best” is never value-neutral. Greedy methods depend on how we define cost, benefit, or coverage, and those definitions inevitably privilege certain stakeholders over others. For the in-class activities, we will first teach student the basic theory of the greedy algorithm, and then let them do fundamental reflection on example such as the interval-cover irrigation lab.

The programming assignment extends this realization into a full system, modeled on real rideshare routing. Students implement Dijkstra’s algorithm for multiple stakeholders, explore what breaks when assumptions such as non-negative weights are violated, and finally modify the algorithm around a chosen ethical concern—fatigue, fairness, or weather safety. In doing so, they see that greedy paths are not universal truths: they emerge from the priorities we encode, the constraints we notice (or overlook), and the people we choose to protect.

Together, the in-class activities and assignment help students understand greedy algorithms as both technical tools and sites of responsibility. And efficient solutions must be balanced with transparency, fairness, and awareness of real-world impact.
